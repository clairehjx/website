Under the hood

How Shophouse Row
works

My biggest project: a little pixel town where my friends each run a shop on one shared street. Here's what's actually going on behind the cuteness — and it's less magic than you'd think.

What you actually do

There's no score and no winning — it's a cozy hangout. The loop is simple:

  • Log in and land on the shared street.
  • See which friends are around — their avatars stand outside their shops.
  • Pop into your own shophouse to redecorate and restock.
  • Wander over to a friend's shop and browse their stuff.
  • Trade an item, leave a short note, or drop off a gift.
  • Come back later to see what's changed.

Everyone runs a different kind of shop, so the street feels like a proper little town:

BakeryFloristBookshopBubble TeaArt StudioPet ShopSweet ShopMusic ShopToy ShopGarden Shop+ more

The four pieces

The whole game is really just four things working together.

React + ViteThe screens, buttons and menus you tap.
Canvas pixel artEvery sprite drawn by hand in code — no game engine.
SupabaseA database in the cloud where everyone's stuff lives.
Vercel functionsThe safe middle-man that checks every change.
Step by step

What's going on inside

1

It's all just tiny coloured squares

src/pixel/engine.js

Every character, shop and item is a little sprite — basically a list of text rows where each letter stands for a colour. My code reads the letters and paints the squares onto a Canvas, then blows them up 4× so you can actually see them.

HTML Canvasno game engine

Why: drawing pixels myself, square by square, means I understand exactly how everything looks — and I get to draw all the art too. (Slower to build, but it's mine.)

2

You walk around a drawn world

src/screens/Street.jsx · components/ShopRoom.jsx

The street and each shop are grids of tiles. The screen is re-drawn about 60 times a second, the camera follows your character, and you can "enter" a shop once you've walked close enough to the door.

60 fps looptop-down tiles

Why: redrawing everything every frame is how the walking looks smooth — like a tiny Pokémon town.

3

You draw your own things

src/components/PixelEditor.jsx

There's a little pixel editor — now a Floor Tile Designer: start from a pattern (wood, checker, brick, water…) or just paint your own, name it, and it becomes a floor tile you can walk on. It's saved as a sprite (the same list-of-rows format as everything else) in a shared creations table, so when you trade or gift one, your friend gets the exact tile you drew.

player-made artshared registry

Why: the stuff people make themselves is the best part, so it had to be sharable — and now you can floor your whole shop with it.

4

Decorate your shophouse

src/components/ShopRoom.jsx · pixel/roomEngine.js · pixel/themes.js

The rooms got a big(ish) upgrade. They're proper tiled rooms now (basically the inside of a building in Pokémon Gold), and you can recolour the whole room with a theme. Furniture you drag, drop and rotate — and it's solid, so you walk around the couch instead of straight through it. Your own pixel drawings lie flat as floor tiles; furniture sits on top. Each placement is now just { item, x, y } — an exact pixel spot, not a clunky grid square.

drag & rotatesolid furnitureroom themes

The one rule I refused to break: never lose a decoration anyone had already placed. So old grid-square decorations quietly convert to the new pixel positions next time you save — same spot, nothing thrown away. (Still two floors, and the upstairs is still your secret.)

5

Logging in (safely)

api/login.js

You log in with your name + a secret code. The code is scrambled with a one-way hash, so the real code is never stored anywhere — not even I can read it. If it matches, you get a token that keeps you logged in.

hashed codesfixed friend list

Why: it's only my ~14 friends — no open sign-ups — so nobody random can wander in. Safety first.

6

How everyone shares one street

api/rpc.js · api/_lib.js · supabase/schema.sql

Everyone's shops, items and notes live in one Supabase database in the cloud. Your game never touches it directly — it asks a Vercel function, which checks you're allowed to do the thing, then makes the change. That way nobody can cheat by editing things they shouldn't.

Supabaseserver-checked

Being honest: it's not live yet — every 60 seconds your game says "I'm still here", and a friend counts as online if they've been seen in the last 5 minutes. Proper real-time updates are on my TODO list.

7

Notes, trades and gifts

src/screens/Inbox.jsx · Trades.jsx

Three little database tables run all the social bits: messages (sticky notes, kept short), trades (you offer an item, your friend accepts, and both items swap at the same moment), and gifts (one-way — it even posts a note saying who it's from).

trade = atomic swapgifts

Why: swapping both items together means a trade can't go halfway and lose someone's stuff.

8

One game, two brains

src/data/api.js

My favourite trick: the whole game can run two ways behind a single switch. On my laptop it stores everything in the browser (no internet needed), and online it uses Supabase — and the screens never know the difference.

offline-firstone clean seam

Why: I can build and test the whole thing on a train with no wifi, then flip one switch to go live. Breaking the problem into "the game" and "where it saves" made everything simpler.

The rules I gave myself

It's built for a specific group of friends, so I added guardrails on purpose: a fixed list of players, no open chat (just short notes), and every change checked on the server so nothing can be faked.

And there's no winning — no score, no leaderboard. It's just for hanging out, decorating, and trading. That part was the whole point.

How I built it

What I told the AI to make

I didn't just say "make me a game". I wrote a proper plan first, then handed it to my AI piece by piece. These were the key instructions:

Build a cozy pixel-art game where everyone owns a shophouse on one shared street — decorate your shop, visit friends, trade items, and leave notes. No winning, just vibes.
Use React, Supabase and Vercel — and draw the pixel art on a Canvas myself. No game engine.
Keep it private: a fixed list of my friends, no sign-ups. Log in with a name and a secret code, and store the codes hashed — never as plain text.
No open chat — only short sticky notes (100 characters max), so it stays safe for a group of 11-year-olds.
Let players trade: I offer one of my items for one of yours; you accept or decline; if you accept, the two items swap automatically.
Make an avatar creator from preset pixel parts — skin, hair, outfit, accessory — and draw all the sprites in a soft, pastel pixel-art style.
Make it installable on a phone like a real app, and still playable offline.
