Lesson 1 · only PSLE maths inside

How AI works (and what
an “LLM” really is)

Everyone says AI is super complicated. Mostly it isn't — the heart of it is maths I already know: multiplying, adding, percentages, averages, and the guess-and-check trick from school. A tool like the one I build with is really just a giant next-word guesser. Every number below, you can check yourself — and by the end you'll know exactly what those three letters, LLM, stand for.

1
🔮 the big idea

It's just guessing the next word

When you type "The cat sat on the ___", the AI doesn't actually know the answer. It just guesses which word comes next — then adds it, guesses the next one, and the next, until there's a whole sentence. That's the big secret: one little guess at a time.

2
✖️➕ multiply, add, compare

It gives each word points — out of 100

To score a word, it checks the word against a few clues and gives each clue a mark out of 10 — how well the word fits that clue. Each clue also has an importance number (grown-ups call it a "weight") that says how much that clue counts. A word's points = each mark × its clue's importance, all added together. That's the whole sum.

Let's try "The cat sat on the ___" with just two clues — and we'll say both clues matter the same, importance 5:

Clue A — does it fit "sat on the…"?  ·  Clue B — does it sound like a sentence about a cat?

WordClue A markClue B markPoints = 5×A + 5×B
mat845×8 + 5×4 = 60
dog425×4 + 5×2 = 30
sky115×1 + 5×1 = 10

Walk through mat: it fits "on" really well, so Clue A scores 8; it's fairly cat-ish, so Clue B scores 4. Multiply each mark by the importance (5) and add: 5×8 + 5×4 = 40 + 20 = 60. Do the same for the others, and mat comes out on top. Just multiplying and adding — no magic.

To compare the three easily, turn the points into percentages ("out of 100"). They already add up to 60 + 30 + 10 = 100, so mat = 60%, dog = 30%, sky = 10% — and the highest usually wins.

3
🚪 the gate & teamwork

Two bits I skipped: a gate, and teamwork

Real models add one move to that points sum. After everything's added up, the total goes through a tiny rule called an activation function — basically a gate. The simplest one just says: "if the total comes out negative, call it 0; otherwise keep it." Weak signals get blocked; strong ones pass through.

Why bother? Because adding sums on top of sums is still just… one big sum — a straight line. The gate lets the model bend, so it can catch trickier patterns, like "only count Clue B if Clue A is there too."

And the importance numbers don't work alone. The answer from one little sum becomes a clue for the next batch of sums — layer after layer, like a relay race. So the numbers team up: nudge one and everything downstream shifts. That teaming-up is where most of the cleverness hides.

4
🔁 guess & check

How did it get good? Guess and check — millions of times

Here's the clever part: those importance numbers didn't start out smart — they began as random guesses. The AI reads a real sentence where it already knows the true next word, sees how wrong it was, and nudges the numbers a little less wrong. Then again. And again. It's literally the guess-and-check method from maths class — just done millions of times.

5
➗ average

Why it reads SO much

To get those numbers right, it can't just learn from one sentence — one sentence might be weird or unusual. So it reads a huge number of them and basically takes an average, so the usual next word wins instead of a strange one-off.

For example: if "mat" came next in 8 out of 10 cat-sentences, that's a strong average — so "mat" is the safe guess. More examples → a steadier average → better guesses.

6
🚀 scale

It's the same maths — just BIGGER

A real AI tool does exactly this: list the choices, multiply each clue by its importance, add them up, turn it into percentages, and learn the importance numbers by guess-and-check. The only difference is how many numbers — not 2 clues, but millions of them.

7
🧩 model = a good-enough copy

So what's an "LLM"? Start with M — Model

People throw the word "LLM" around like everyone just knows it. It's three words — Large Language Model — and you've basically already met it. Start at the end: a model is a simplified copy of something real that's still useful — a model aeroplane, or the weather model that guesses if it'll rain. Not the whole thing, just enough to work. An LLM is a model of how language usually goes — a copy of the patterns in writing.

8
📈 large means LARGE

The big L is for Large — and it means it

It's large in two ways: it learned from a giant pile of writing — billions of words — and it holds a giant pile of those importance numbers from the steps above, millions to billions of them.

How big is a billion, really? If you read one word a second and never stopped to sleep:

1,000,000,000 words ÷ 1 per second ≈ 32 YEARS

…just to read a billion words once. An LLM trains on way more than that. So yes — large.

9
🧠 put it together

Stick the three words back together

The middle L is for Language — it works in words: you give it some, it gives some back, exactly the next-word guessing from the steps above. So an LLM is a Model (a good-enough copy) of Language (words), made Large (tons of writing, tons of importance numbers). That's it — the same guesser from Step 1, scaled up enormously. That scale is the whole reason it can write stories, answer questions, and help me code.

10
⚠️ it doesn't actually KNOW

What it ISN'T

The bit people forget: an LLM doesn't truly know facts, and it isn't thinking like a person. It's guessing the most likely-sounding words — which means it can be confidently, cheerfully WRONG. (My perpetually cheerful AI "pet" will happily tell me complete nonsense with a straight face.) So I always double-check it. No feelings, no magic — just maths.

So "AI" and "LLM" aren't magic spells

The maths I learn at school — multiplying, adding, percentages, averages, and guess-and-check — really is the engine inside AI. And "LLM" is just three honest words: a Large Language Model. Same small sums as Step 1, only an absolutely enormous number of them. Clever? Definitely. Magic? No. Actually thinking? Also no.

Lesson 2 · still only PSLE maths

What's the difference
between models?

Claude comes in three sizes — Haiku, Sonnet, Opus. Gemini too — Flash-Lite, Flash, Pro. (Yes, the names are a lot.) They're not three different kinds of magic — they're the same next-word machine from Lesson 1, built in three sizes. Knowing which one to grab is mostly about one thing: money.

1
🏷️ small · medium · big

Every brand sells the same three sizes

Once you spot the pattern you can't unsee it. Each company sells a small, a medium, and a big version of its model:

Claude: Haiku · Sonnet · Opus Gemini: Flash-Lite · Flash · Pro

Left = small (quick, cheap, less clever). Right = big (slower, pricier, cleverer). Same idea, two brands. The catch: you don't always want the big one — see the last step.

2
✖️➕ more sums

The maths difference: how many sums

Remember the importance numbers from Lesson 1 — the ones you multiply and add to score each word? A bigger model simply has WAY more of them. More numbers means more multiplying-and-adding for every single word it guesses. More sums → cleverer guesses, but also slower and more expensive. That's the whole trade: bigger brain, bigger electricity bill.

3
🧩 tokens

What's a "token"?

A model doesn't read whole words — it chops text into tokens, little word-ish chunks. A short word like "cat" is 1 token; a long one like "watercolour" is a few; spaces and full stops count too.

rough rule: ~4 letters ≈ 1 token

And here's the money bit: you pay per token — for what you put IN (your question) and what it writes OUT (its answer).

4
💰 one more multiply

Why one costs more than another

Your bill is just another multiply you already know how to do:

how many tokens × price per token = cost

Two things push it up: more tokens (a long question and a long answer), and a bigger model — which charges more per token, because each token drags along all those extra sums from Step 2. Put together, the big model can cost roughly:

big ≈ 10–30× the small one, per word

5
🧠 a second dial: thinking level

What "changing the thinking level" means

Model size isn't the only dial. Most newer models also let you set a thinking level — how much working-out the model does before it answers. It's the difference between blurting "7 × 8 = 56" off the top of your head and showing your full working on a hard word problem.

Turn it down (some call the lowest setting "minimal") and it just gives its first guess — fast and cheap. Turn it up ("high") and it first scribbles loads of hidden working-out — extra thinking tokens, which (remember Step 4) you also pay for — and that makes it much better at tricky problems, but slower and pricier.

Same brain either way. You're only choosing how hard it bothers to think — so you can save the deep thinking for the jobs that actually need it.

6
🧮 right-size it

How I keep my AI bill down

This is exactly what I had to think about in my Science Tutoring Tool. Four tricks:

1. Pick the smallest model that's good enough. I used Flash, not Pro — Pro is cleverer than building a cheat sheet actually needs.

2. Turn thinking DOWN on the easy steps. Checking a question barely needs thought; only the writing step thinks hard.

3. Keep questions short — fewer tokens IN.

4. CACHE. Once my tool makes a cheat sheet, it saves it — so running it again costs nothing. Never pay twice for the same answer.

(All of that is on the how my tool works page.)

So bigger isn't "better" — it's just bigger

The skill isn't grabbing the biggest, cleverest model every time. It's matching the model — and how hard it thinks — to the job in front of you. Which, when you think about it, is just problem solving. (My perpetually cheerful AI "pet" would happily fire up Opus to work out 2 + 2. I would not.)
