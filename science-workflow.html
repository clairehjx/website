🔬 Under the hood

How my Science
Tutoring Tool works

My tool turns real exam papers into friendly cheat sheets in a few steps. The neat part: each step uses an AI thinking level that matches how hard the job is — fast for the easy bits, deep for the clever bits.

🧠 What's a "thinking level"?

Newer AI models can choose how hard to think before answering. I pick the level on purpose for each step — more thinking is smarter but slower and costs more, so I save it for the tricky parts.

MinimalBarely stops to think. Fastest & cheapest — great for simple checking.
MediumA balanced amount of thinking for everyday jobs.
HighThinks really hard. Saved for the most creative, tricky work.
Step by step

The recipe my tool follows 🧾

1

Read the exam papers

pdf_extractor.py

It looks at each real exam PDF and pulls out the open-ended questions, their model answers, and any diagrams.

Gemini 3.5 Flash medium thinking

Why: This step has to see the page (that's called "vision") and copy the words exactly. I pin it at medium — enough thinking to sort multi-part questions into the right order, but not so much that it starts "improving" the wording instead of copying it.

2

Check every question

pipeline.py

For each question it asks "Does this really match the topic?" and "Is the answer correct?" The bad ones get dropped.

Gemini 3.5 Flash medium thinking

Why: This step can drop a question or even rewrite a model answer, so getting it right really matters — and it runs as just one batched call per cheat sheet, so extra thinking is almost free here. I set it to medium so it weighs topic-match and answer-correctness carefully.

3

Write the cheat sheet

pipeline.py

The big one! It turns the questions into the 7 tabs — common mistakes, tricky differences, keywords, answer formulas and the summary.

Gemini 3.5 Flash high thinking

Why: This is the cleverest, most creative step. Explaining science clearly for a kid, and spotting the exact mistakes and confusions, takes real thought — so I turn thinking all the way up to high.

4

Add the real question pictures

top_questions_images.py

It snips the actual question straight out of the PDF as a picture and puts it on the "Top Questions" tab.

No AI — just code Gemini 3.5 Flash (backup)

Why: Cutting out a picture at known coordinates is exact maths, so plain code does it — no AI needed, which is faster and free. The AI (vision) only steps in as a backup if a question is hard to find.

5

Plan the summary picture

image_generator.py

It writes a detailed "art brief" describing the big concept picture for the summary tab.

Gemini 3.5 Flash medium thinking

Why: Designing a good picture brief needs some thinking, but not the heaviest — so medium is the balanced middle setting.

6

Draw the picture

image_generator.py

It actually draws the watercolour-style summary picture from the brief.

nano-banana · Gemini image model

Why: This is the one job that makes pictures, so it uses Google's special image-drawing model instead of the text one. Thinking levels don't apply here.

7

Make a practice quiz (optional)

oe_quiz_generator.py

It builds a printable practice quiz by picking the hardest questions and cropping them from the PDF.

Gemini 3.5 Flash standard thinking · vision

Why: Like step 1, it just needs to see the page and find each question — light work for the fast Flash model.

🛟 What if a model is busy?

If Gemini 3.5 Flash is ever too busy to answer, every step automatically falls back to a slightly older model, Gemini 3 Flash, so my tool never gets stuck.

💡 Why one model nearly everywhere?

I use one fast, friendly model — Gemini 3.5 Flash — for almost everything, and just change its thinking level to match how hard each step is. That keeps the tool quick and affordable, while still thinking hard exactly where it counts.
